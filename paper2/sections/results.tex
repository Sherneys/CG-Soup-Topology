\subsection{Gate: the loss alone repairs topology}
Before integration, the loss (no photometric term, Adam directly on point
positions) must repair defective clouds. It does, on all four probes
(Fig.~\ref{fig:toys}):
% src: figures/phase3_toy/t{1..4}.json (PHASE3_PLAN.md Appendix A)
a punctured sphere's void birth is pulled to target ($8.8\times$ bottleneck
reduction; the hole closes), a spurious handle is pushed to the diagonal and
dies (H1 bar eliminated; $\beta$ restored), mis-spaced components are pulled
to the target separation ($1229\times$), and --- the recruitment probe --- a
bridged merge with \emph{no} significant live H0 bar is severed
($450\times$, $\beta_0\,1{\to}2$). The predicted location-blind pathology did
not materialize, for a mechanism reason worth stating: tearing a 2-manifold
shell cannot grow a component bar past the local detour scale (the tug is
abandoned), while cutting the 1-D bridge raises the recruited bar's death
monotonically toward target --- the runaway concentrates on the only
profitable direction.

\paragraph{Voids (H2): topology-specific, large.} Sphere $4.0\times$
($16.2\sigma$ Welch vs C0), cube $7.9\times$ ($18.5\sigma$), both at
equal-or-better Chamfer (sphere $0.46$ vs $0.50$; cube $0.92$ vs $1.05$). The
norm-matched control is not merely weaker --- it is \emph{destructive}: it
pins the void's death at its repulsion equilibrium (bottleneck $2.2\times$
worse than baseline, identical across seeds) and on the cube erases the void
outright. Nor is this an artifact of control strength: a gentle variant at
half the push radius still lands $1.6\times$ \emph{worse} than baseline
(.0972 vs .0623) --- the sphere ordering is C1 $<$ C0 $<$ C2g $<$ C2, so no
tested repulsion strength reproduces any of the gain
(Fig.~\ref{fig:tails}).

\paragraph{Loops (H1): the channel's distinguishing result.} In the
allocation-channel study (Appendix~\ref{app:companion}) no prior shape won
this class --- concentration manufactured phantom handles; spreading never
beat a random control. The loss cuts torus
H1 error $2.3\times$ with \emph{zero phantom handles in all five seeds}
(significant-count trajectories flat at the true $\beta_1{=}2$,
Fig.~\ref{fig:nsig}), while the control collapses one loop. Metric pressure
on birth/death values suits 1-D features in a way budget allocation does not.

\paragraph{Components (H0): not topological --- third channel, same answer.}
C1 reduces the (already small) baseline error $9.5\times$, but the generic
control matches it on the diagram metric (.0008 vs .0007); the topological
increment appears only in Chamfer ($0.54$ vs $1.94$). Together with the
allocation-channel results this is now a statement about the feature class:
component structure is helped by any spreading pressure, not by topological
information.

\paragraph{Composition (C5).} Loss $+$ spread prior is best everywhere
tested --- sphere $.0082$ ($7.6\times$ vs C0, ${\approx}2\times$ vs C1 alone,
${\approx}4.6\times$ below the prior alone
% src: h2_unified/report/results.json (B4 sphere ~.038)
), torus $.0133$ --- with the best sphere Chamfer as well; on the torus its
Chamfer ($.502$) trails only the no-ramp arm ($.495$). Allocation and
value-tuning are complementary mechanisms, not substitutes.
% src: topo3/report/results.json (chamfer_mean per arm)

\paragraph{Stretch case (genus 2): saturated, honestly.} On the double torus
every arm --- baseline, loss, control --- returns a bottleneck of exactly
$.0264$ (sd $0$ over seeds), and Wasserstein is equally pinned
($.0527$--$.0528$): the two tube loops are unexpressable at feasible budgets
(replicating the companion study's floor), and they dominate both diagram
metrics regardless of guidance. The loss adds no phantoms there
($\beta_1$-significant count $=2$ in every run) and its Chamfer is marginally
best; the case measures the \emph{representation's} ceiling, not the
channel's.
% src: topo3/report/{results.json,cache/double_torus_*} (W2 check 2026-07-07)

\paragraph{Ablations.} $\rho$-response flat over $\{0.03,0.1,0.3\}$
(.0181/.0172/.0177 on sphere); C3 (no curriculum) $\approx$ C1 on both
decisive shapes. Overhead: ${\approx}45$--$55$\,s per 2{,}500-step run
(refresh 174\,ms at $M{=}2048$, every 10 steps).

\subsection{Generality: external genus-known meshes}
\label{sec:generality}
Everything above is on one analytic scene family. We therefore repeat the
verdict protocol --- C0/C1/C2, three seeds, class budgets, $\rho$, ramp and
sampling unchanged --- on three external watertight meshes of known genus
(\S\ref{sec:setup}): \emph{spot} (genus 0, organic), \emph{bob} (genus 1,
organic), and the \emph{fandisk} CAD benchmark (genus 0). No per-shape
tuning of any kind: the headroom budgets transfer from the feature class,
and bob runs the full unrestricted bundle.
% src: topo3/report/results.json (spot/bob/fandisk aggregates + verdicts)
All three \textbf{pass} the verdict rule at equal-or-\emph{better} Chamfer
(Table~\ref{tab:gen}, Fig.~\ref{fig:gen}), and the control's failure modes
replicate exactly: repulsion erases the void on both H2 shapes
($\beta_2\,1{\to}0$, all seeds) and collapses a loop on the genus-1 shape
($\beta_1\,2{\to}1$, all seeds).

Three observations. \emph{The H1 claim survives the family change}: bob
halves loop error with zero phantom handles in every seed ($26.1\sigma$),
replicating torus. \emph{Sharp-featured CAD benefits most}: fandisk's
$10.4\times$ exceeds every analytic-family effect. \emph{Complex organic
geometry exposes a count-level limit}: spot's \emph{baseline} manufactures a
phantom void in two of three seeds (final $\#$sig H2 $= 2,1,2$); the loss
clears it in two seeds but retains one spurious bar in the third ($1,2,1$)
--- value repair is reliable, count repair on sub-budget organic detail
(legs, horns at $N{=}1200$) is an improvement, not a guarantee. Spot's C1
tail is also pinned across seeds (sd $0$ at $.0237$), suggesting a geometric
floor of the same kind as the double torus, far milder in degree.
